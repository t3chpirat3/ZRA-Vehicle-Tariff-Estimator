Introduction and Geopolitical Context
Zambia occupies a highly strategic geographical position within the Southern African
Development Community (SADC) and the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa
(COMESA). Bordered by eight sovereign nations—including the Democratic Republic of the
Congo to the north, Tanzania to the northeast, Malawi and Mozambique to the east, Zimbabwe
and Botswana to the south, Namibia to the southwest, and Angola to the west—the country
functions as the primary logistical keystone of the North-South Corridor.1 This geographic
reality facilitates the extensive movement of commercial freight, overland tourism, and regional
passenger transport across the continent.1 Consequently, the regulatory mechanisms
governing the cross-border movement of motor vehicles are critical to both national security
and domestic economic stability.
The Temporary Importation Permit (TIP) and the corresponding Customs Import Permit (CIP)
serve as the primary legal instruments enabling foreign nationals, tourists, and commercial
operators to bring foreign-registered vehicles into Zambian territory without incurring the
prohibitive costs of permanent importation.3 The temporary importation framework is
meticulously designed to balance two competing sovereign interests: the facilitation of
seamless international trade and tourism, and the strict prevention of customs fraud. Without a
robust TIP framework, the Zambian automotive market would be highly susceptible to the illicit
influx of undocumented, untaxed foreign vehicles, which would undermine domestic revenue
collection and disrupt the local automotive economy.5 Furthermore, the procedures
surrounding the TIP intersect with broader national macroeconomic policies, including
environmental sustainability via carbon taxation, infrastructure maintenance through
sophisticated road tolling networks, and regional risk management via compulsory third-party
insurance schemes.7 This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level analysis of the procedural
steps, documentation prerequisites, financial architectures, and strict statutory limitations
governing the temporary importation of foreign-registered vehicles into the Republic of
Zambia.
Legislative and Regulatory Underpinnings
The legal foundation for the temporary importation of goods and motor vehicles into Zambia is
anchored in Section 89 of the Customs and Excise Act, functioning in tandem with Regulation
78 of the Customs and Excise (General) Regulations of 2000.4 This legislative architecture
provides the Commissioner-General of the Zambia Revenue Authority (ZRA) with the statutory
--- PAGE 2 ---
authority to grant a rebate or remission of duty for goods temporarily imported into the
country, provided the duration of stay does not exceed the authorized period and all statutory
conditions are met.4
A fundamental eligibility criterion built into this legislation is that the vehicle or goods subject to
the application must be unequivocally owned by a natural or corporate person resident outside
the Republic of Zambia.4 This stipulation serves as a critical anti-dumping and anti-smuggling
measure. By restricting temporary relief to bona fide non-residents, the Zambian government
effectively mitigates the risk of its own citizens utilizing the TIP framework to bypass the
standard import duties, excise duties, and Value Added Tax (VAT) applied to permanent vehicle
imports.4
The legislative framework categorizes temporary importations into two distinct relief
paradigms: total conditional relief and partial conditional relief.4 Motor vehicles imported
temporarily by tourists, visitors, and transiting travelers generally qualify for total conditional
relief, meaning they are completely exempt from paying the proportionate depreciated value of
the vehicle's duty, provided the vehicle is used exclusively for personal transport, frontier
traffic, or approved developmental programs, and is exported prior to the permit's expiration.4
The regulations explicitly and strictly prohibit the loaning, renting, or selling of these vehicles
within Zambian territory without prior written consent from Customs authorities.4 Conversely,
partial conditional relief applies to vehicles imported under commercial hire, lease, or
related-party contractor arrangements, where import duties may be levied based on the
proportionate depreciated value of the asset over its authorized stay in the country, assuming a
maximum lifespan of five years for depreciation purposes.4
The Financial Alternative: Contextualizing Permanent
Importation Costs
To fully comprehend the rigorous scrutiny applied to Temporary Importation Permits by the
Zambia Revenue Authority, it is necessary to analyze the alternative: the financial burden of
permanent vehicle importation. Zambia does not enforce a strict age-based import ban like
some neighboring jurisdictions, but it heavily penalizes the importation of older vehicles
through a cascading taxation system based on the Cost, Insurance, and Freight (CIF) value of
the vehicle.10
When a vehicle is imported permanently, it is subject to a standard Customs Duty, which is
typically calculated at 25 percent of the CIF value for passenger sedans and up to 30 percent
for station wagons and Sport Utility Vehicles (SUVs).11 Following the application of the Customs
Duty, an Excise Duty is levied. For passenger vehicles with an engine capacity not exceeding
1500cc, this excise duty is 20 percent of the combined CIF and Customs Duty value.12 For
vehicles exceeding 1500cc, the excise rate escalates to 30 percent.11 Furthermore, a flat Value
Added Tax (VAT) of 16 percent is applied to the cumulative total of the CIF, Customs Duty, and
Excise Duty.11
Beyond these standard taxes, the Zambian government imposes a Motor Vehicle Surtax on any
vehicle older than five years at the time of importation, acting as a deliberate disincentive to
--- PAGE 3 ---
prevent the country from becoming a dumping ground for aged, highly polluting automobiles.11
This surtax adds an additional 20 percent penalty based on the vehicle's value.11 Consequently,
the total landed cost of permanently importing a vehicle into Zambia often results in a
cumulative tax burden ranging from 45 percent to well over 60 percent of the original Free on
Board (FOB) price of the asset.10 Given this massive financial liability, the incentive for illicit
actors to smuggle vehicles into the country under the guise of a Temporary Import Permit is
immense. The exhaustive procedural steps, documentation requirements, and draconian
penalties discussed throughout this report are directly engineered by the ZRA to protect the
domestic tax base from this specific vector of customs fraud.
The Digital Customs Infrastructure: ASYCUDAWorld
and the IBDF
The operational procedure for clearing a foreign-registered vehicle at a Zambian border post
has undergone significant digital transformation over the past decade, transitioning from highly
fragmented, paper-based bureaucratic processes to unified, digital declarations. At the core of
this modernization is the Zambia Revenue Authority's implementation of the ASYCUDAWorld
system, a globally recognized automated customs data management platform.1 The
deployment of ASYCUDAWorld has enabled the integration of multiple border agencies into a
more cohesive, transparent, and auditable workflow, culminating in the mandatory utilization of
the Integrated Border Declaration Form (IBDF).18
The IBDF is a sophisticated, multi-purpose customs instrument designed to streamline the
passenger and vehicle clearance process. The form is utilized simultaneously for the temporary
entry of motor vehicles imported by visitors and tourists for their own use, the entry of
personal motor vehicles transiting through Zambia to third-party countries, the declaration of
visitors' accompanied baggage stored within the vehicle, and the mandatory financial
declaration of any physical currency carried by the traveler in excess of the equivalent of USD
5,000.18 By consolidating these disparate declarations into a single digital node, the ZRA has
significantly reduced the administrative friction previously associated with crossing Zambian
borders, while concurrently enhancing data collection for the Electronic Balance of Payments
(e-BOP) system and national security watchlists.17
Recent enhancements to the ASYCUDAWorld platform and the IBDF architecture have
introduced mandatory electronic attachment capabilities, requiring travelers to digitally upload
critical supporting documents—such as third-party insurance certificates, driver identification,
and vehicle registration logs—directly into the system during the clearance process.16 To
facilitate this transition for self-driving tourists and overlanders who may lack prior knowledge
of the digital requirements, the ZRA has actively deployed dedicated IBDF computer terminals
at major One-Stop Border Posts (OSBPs) like Kazungula, alongside specialized counters for
Interpol verification within the passenger terminals.19
Procedural Workflow for Border Clearance
The clearance process for a foreign-registered vehicle requires meticulous adherence to a
structured, multi-step workflow upon physical arrival at the Zambian port of entry. This process
--- PAGE 4 ---
is generally standardized across all major border posts, although localized logistical variations
may exist at specialized One-Stop Border Posts.18
The first phase of the clearance protocol entails the primary declaration of information and
immigration processing. Before addressing vehicle importation, the driver and all passengers
must proceed to the Department of Immigration to acquire the necessary entry visas, which
may range from standard 30-day tourist visas to specialized 90-day business authorizations,
depending on the traveler's nationality and purpose of visit.21 Once passports are officially
stamped, the traveler proceeds to the customs administration desk to initiate the IBDF process.
Here, the driver must provide comprehensive personal identification details, precise particulars
of the motor vehicle (including engine capacity, chassis number, and registration data), travel
itineraries, and a declaration of any accompanied goods.18 Concurrently, the driver is required
to complete a physical inspection form for the motor vehicle.18
The second phase involves the rigorous assessment of the declaration by ZRA Customs
officers. During this stage, customs officials utilize the ASYCUDAWorld system to evaluate the
vehicle's parameters, determine the applicable environmental levies based on the declared
engine capacity, and verify the physical vehicle against the provided documentation.18 A critical
component of this assessment phase is the Interpol clearance check. The vehicle's Vehicle
Identification Number (VIN) and engine serial numbers are cross-referenced against regional
and international stolen vehicle databases.19 This physical inspection and digital verification are
crucial for intercepting stolen vehicles attempting to transit through the Zambian corridor to
black markets in the Democratic Republic of the Congo or East Africa.
The third phase is the financial settlement of all assessed taxes, tolls, and levies. Upon
completion of the assessment, the ASYCUDA system generates a consolidated financial
demand. The traveler is required to proceed to the designated cashier or digital payment
terminal to remit payment for all applicable fees simultaneously.18 The ZRA has increasingly
integrated electronic payment platforms across its border networks, allowing travelers to utilize
credit and debit facilities.17 However, the reliability of local network infrastructure can be
volatile, making it an established best practice for overlanders and logistics operators to carry
sufficient physical cash in both United States Dollars (USD) and Zambian Kwacha (ZMW), as
specific levies require payment in distinct currencies.20
The fourth and final phase is the formal issuance of the IBDF and associated permits. Upon
verification of cleared funds, Customs issues a formal receipt of payment and a physical
printout of the IBDF, which serves as the official, legally binding Temporary Import Permit for
the vehicle.18 For certain extended stays or specific commercial classifications, drivers may also
be required to interface with the Road Transport and Safety Agency (RTSA) digital portal.
Through this integrated "My Workplace" portal, an applicant can digitally sign their application
using a PIN-code, submit it for a final physical examination at a designated RTSA station, and
subsequently receive a Temporary Motor Vehicle Registration Certificate, which remains
permanently archived in the user's digital workplace for future compliance checks or localized
renewals.27
Exhaustive Documentation Requirements
--- PAGE 5 ---
The success of a temporary importation application hinges entirely on the presentation of
comprehensive, unaltered, and authenticated documentation. The Zambian border authorities
operate under strict legislative mandates, making documentary compliance the most heavily
scrutinized element of the entire border crossing experience.22
The primary application document for formal, non-tourist temporary imports is the prescribed
Form TIP 0001, obtainable at any Customs House.4 The completion of this form requires
granular detail, including the physical address of the applicant, the exact reasons necessitating
the temporary importation, the intended duration of stay, and specific identification details of
the vehicle.4 If the vehicle is leased or hired, the applicant must also disclose the terms and
conditions under which the vehicle was sourced from the supplier, alongside any form of
financial security for duties and taxes that Customs may require to mitigate flight risk.4
According to ZRA guidelines, completed application forms for commercial TIPs should be
submitted to the Station Manager of the intended port of entry at least five days before the
intended date of arrival.4
Beyond the formal application form, the applicant must present a suite of supporting
documents to validate ownership, identity, and intent. The original vehicle registration book
(frequently referred to as the logbook) is strictly mandatory.2 While some remote border posts
may occasionally accept highly authenticated, certified copies under exceptional
circumstances, presenting the original registration documentation is the established and
necessary standard to avoid severe delays or outright rejection of entry.2
If the driver is not the formally registered owner of the vehicle, the evidentiary burden
increases significantly to combat unauthorized exportation. In such instances, the driver must
produce a legally binding affidavit or formal letter, certified by a recognized Commissioner of
Oaths or police authority, explicitly granting authorization from the registered owner to take
the vehicle across international borders into Zambian territory.2 Furthermore, if the vehicle
remains under a financing agreement with a bank or commercial financial institution, the driver
must possess a formal letter of authority from that specific financier.2 This letter must explicitly
state the approved dates of international travel and be accompanied by the certified vehicle
license papers.2
A Police Clearance Certificate, typically issued by regional Interpol bureaus, is a highly critical
requirement for vehicles and trailers crossing from neighboring SADC countries, particularly
South Africa and Zimbabwe.2 This certificate validates that the vehicle is not listed on any
regional stolen vehicle registries. While recent regional harmonization efforts and shifting
bilateral agreements have occasionally relaxed this requirement for standard, short-term
holiday travel in specific contexts, possessing a valid police clearance remains the most secure
and recommended method to ensure uninterrupted border transit.28
Personal documentation requirements are equally rigorous. The driver must present a valid
passport containing the appropriate visas or entry stamps.2 From a vehicular operation
standpoint, the driver must hold a valid domestic driver's license accompanied by an
International Driving Permit (IDP).2 The IDP serves as a universally recognized translation of the
national driving license and is a strict legal prerequisite for operating a motor vehicle on
--- PAGE 6 ---
Zambian public roads.28 Commercial entrants must also provide documents supporting the
nature of their business transaction in Zambia, such as lease agreements, localized
employment contracts, or temporary exportation documents originating from the vehicle's
home country.4
The Carnet de Passage en Douane (CPD) Alternative
For overlanders, international tourists, and specific commercial operators, the Carnet de
Passage en Douane (CPD) offers a highly formalized alternative to the standard Zambian TIP.2
The CPD is an internationally recognized customs document that guarantees the payment of
customs duties and taxes to the Zambian government if the vehicle is not re-exported within
the authorized timeframe.29 If a traveler presents a valid Carnet upon entry, the Zambian
customs authority will stamp the document in lieu of issuing a standard localized TIP or IBDF
vehicle permit.21 While the Carnet is not compulsory for vehicles registered in neighboring
countries such as Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Malawi, Zimbabwe, South Africa, Mozambique, and
Botswana, it is highly recommended for vehicles originating from outside the African continent
or for assets intending to remain in Zambia for extended periods exceeding three months, as it
drastically expedites the physical border clearance process and satisfies all ZRA financial
security requirements.2
Border Post Logistics and Spatial Operations
Zambia’s landlocked geography necessitates a vast network of border control posts, each
operating with distinct logistical realities, traffic volumes, and operational hours.1
Understanding the spatial dynamics of these ports of entry is critical for efficient route
planning, as arriving outside of operating hours will strand vehicles in highly congested,
unsecured transit zones.1
The Zambian border network is anchored by several critical One-Stop Border Posts (OSBPs),
which have been engineered to consolidate the exit and entry procedures of two adjoining
nations into a single physical facility, drastically reducing dwell times. The Chirundu OSBP
(bordering Zimbabwe) and the Kazungula OSBP (bordering Botswana) are the most heavily
trafficked southern nodes.1 The Kazungula crossing, in particular, has seen massive
infrastructural upgrades with the completion of the Kazungula Bridge, replacing the antiquated
pontoon ferry system, though localized tolls for bridge access have been introduced.1 To the
north, the Kasumbalesa OSBP serves as the primary, high-volume artery into the Democratic
Republic of the Congo and is notably one of the few border posts operating 24 hours a day to
manage the immense backlog of commercial copper and cobalt freight.1

applied equally to permanently imported assets, local fleet renewals, and foreign vehicles
visiting or transiting through Zambian territory.15 The tax functions as a direct economic
disincentive for highly polluting, older vehicles and serves as a critical revenue generation
mechanism to fund national climate change mitigation and adaptation programs.15

Financial Architecture III: Third-Party Insurance and
Regional Risk Pools
To operate a motor vehicle legally within Zambian territory, the driver must possess valid,
compulsory third-party motor insurance.2 This financial mechanism is a non-negotiable
statutory requirement designed to protect Zambian citizens, local businesses, and national
infrastructure from the immense liabilities that arise from road traffic accidents involving
foreign nationals, who might otherwise exit the jurisdiction without providing adequate
compensation. Drivers entering the country have two primary avenues for achieving
compliance: the localized purchase of border insurance or the utilization of the sophisticated
COMESA Yellow Card scheme.
For motorists arriving without regional coverage, local third-party insurance can be purchased
directly from brokers stationed at the border posts.26 This local policy is typically underwritten
for a standard validity of 30 days and generally costs approximately USD 30, or the equivalent
in Kwacha (historically ranging from K 130 to K 230 depending on the underwriting agency and
the specific vehicle class).20 Upon purchase, the physical insurance disk must be prominently
displayed on the passenger side of the vehicle’s windshield at all times while operating within
the country.20 Failure to display this disk is a primary catalyst for detention at roadside police
checkpoints.
However, the most sophisticated, cost-effective, and seamless method for insurance
compliance across the region is the COMESA Yellow Card.9 The Yellow Card is a highly
integrated, regional third-party motor vehicle insurance scheme recognized by participating
states as unassailable legal evidence of insurance coverage, functioning in strict accordance
with the national laws governing liability in the country where an accident occurs.9 The scheme
operates through an Inter-Bureaux Agreement and a centralized Reinsurance Pool, allowing
motorists to traverse participating nations—including Burundi, Djibouti, Democratic Republic of
Congo, Eritrea, Ethiopia, Kenya, Malawi, Rwanda, Sudan, Tanzania, Uganda, Zambia, and
Zimbabwe—using a single, unified policy.9
The Yellow Card provides comprehensive coverage for bodily injury and death of third parties,
as well as third-party property damage, subject to the maximum limits of liability dictated by
the host nation's legislation.9 Crucially, the scheme offers an extra benefit covering emergency
medical treatment expenses for the driver and passengers of the insured vehicle, drastically
reducing the physical and financial risks associated with severe accidents in remote
transboundary corridors.9 While designed for COMESA member states, the framework
includes strategic Business-to-Business (B2B) arrangements allowing motorists from
non-member SADC countries—most notably South Africa, Mozambique, and Namibia—to
--- PAGE 11 ---
purchase valid Yellow Card coverage from their domestic insurers prior to embarking on their
journeys, thereby bypassing the chaotic and often highly predatory environment of
border-post insurance vendors entirely.9
Financial Architecture IV: Municipal Council Levies
A highly contentious and frequently misunderstood element of the financial architecture is the
imposition of Council Levies by local municipalities stationed at or near major border towns.2
While the central government strictly manages customs taxes and the NRFA administers road
tolls, local district councils—such as those operating in border districts like Siavonga,
Kazungula, and Nakonde—frequently levy their own independent fees on foreign vehicles
entering and exiting their municipal jurisdictions.2 These levies generally amount to USD 20 or
an equivalent Kwacha figure per vehicle and are collected at localized roadblocks shortly after
the primary customs perimeter.2
The stated political and administrative rationale for these levies is that the heavy, continuous
influx of transiting vehicles generates severe localized environmental degradation, overwhelms
municipal waste management capabilities (due to massive amounts of solid and liquid waste
generated by delayed truck drivers), and strains local infrastructure in ways that the centralized
national road fund does not adequately compensate the municipalities for.49 While these
decentralized levies have faced heavy and sustained criticism from regional commercial
logistics bodies (such as FESARTA) for allegedly violating SADC and COMESA transit
agreements regarding the harmonization of transparent road user charges, they remain a de
facto reality on the ground.40 Travelers and commercial operators must be financially and
psychologically prepared to pay these municipal taxes and retain the associated receipts to
avoid local detention of their vehicles.31
Temporal Limitations, Extensions, and Visa
Synchronization
The issuance of a Temporary Importation Permit via the IBDF or a manual Form TIP 0001 is not
an indefinite grant of access. The regulatory framework imposes strict temporal limitations
engineered to prevent the systemic abuse of the system as a backdoor for avoiding permanent
vehicle importation duties.4
The legal parameters of "temporary" vary based on the classification of the entrant. For
standard tourists, holidaymakers, and ordinary visitors entering Zambia on standard visitor
visas, the initial validity of the vehicle's temporary import permit is often synchronized closely
with their immigration status.2 Typically, a tourist is granted a free 30-day vehicle permit upon
entry, mirroring the initial 30 days granted by the Department of Immigration.21 If the visitor
wishes to remain in the country, they can extend their immigration status up to a maximum of
90 days within a rolling 12-month period as an ordinary visitor, and they must simultaneously
proceed to the nearest Customs Office to formally extend the vehicle's IBDF permit to match
this new duration.24 The Zambian government recently extended the allowable stay duration for
visa-exempt business visitors from 30 days to 90 days, providing greater flexibility for
commercial exploratory trips, but the vehicle permits must still be actively managed and
--- PAGE 12 ---
extended to reflect this stay.25
For major commercial entities, expatriates on temporary employment permits, or organizations
involved in approved developmental programs, the maximum initial period allowed for a
foreign-registered vehicle to remain in Zambia under a standard TIP is statutorily capped at
twelve (12) months.4 Upon the expiration of this initial 12-month period, if the vehicle must
remain in the country, the client cannot simply renew the permit at a local customs house.
Instead, they must submit a formal, written application directly to the Commissioner-General
of the ZRA.4 The Commissioner-General possesses the discretionary, non-delegable authority
to extend the maximum allowable period for goods imported for developmental programs, or
for other highly valid reasons, for a subsequent period not exceeding 12 months.4 Crucially, the
total period of all extensions, combined with the initial validity period, is strictly capped at a
maximum of 24 months.4 Beyond this absolute ceiling, the vehicle must be physically exported
from Zambian territory, or it must be formally entered for permanent home consumption,
thereby triggering the immediate assessment of all standard import duties, excise taxes, and
VAT.4
Penalties, Infractions, and Asset Forfeiture Mechanics
The punitive mechanisms designed to enforce the temporal limitations of the TIP are
deliberately draconian. They are calibrated not merely as administrative deterrents, but as
devastating financial consequences engineered to completely eradicate the profit margins
associated with creating a grey market of untaxed foreign vehicles.4
Under the amended provisions of Section 32 of the Customs and Excise Act, any goods or
motor vehicles that remain in Zambia beyond the authorized TIP expiration date automatically
and immediately become liable for the payment of full import duties, excise taxes, and VAT,
stripping away all conditional relief.4 Furthermore, the legislation imposes a crippling daily
statutory fine of 3,000 fee units for every single day (or part thereof) that the vehicle remains
in the country illegally beyond the authorized date.4 In the context of Zambian statutory law,
where penalty units translate directly into substantial Kwacha fines, a vehicle that overstays its
temporary permit by merely a few weeks can rapidly accrue financial penalties that rival or
even exceed the actual market value of the vehicle itself.
The enforcement of this penalty structure is unyielding. If a traveler needs to extend their stay,
the extension must be formalized at a Customs Office before the expiry of the existing IBDF or
TIP; retroactive extensions are not entertained, and penalties apply immediately for the extra
days stayed.51 More severely, the Customs and Excise Act empowers the state with the
authority of asset forfeiture. If any vehicle entered under temporary importation is found to
have exceeded its authorized period by more than ten days without an approved extension, the
vehicle becomes legally liable to immediate seizure by the state.5 This strict enforcement
paradigm, combining massive daily financial penalties with the threat of total asset forfeiture,
underscores the ZRA's absolute commitment to maintaining the integrity of its customs
borders and protecting its national revenue streams.
Physical Safety Regulations and Highway Compliance
--- PAGE 13 ---
Beyond documentary perfection and financial compliance, foreign-registered vehicles must
satisfy stringent physical safety criteria mandated by the Zambian Highway Code, the Road
Transport and Safety Agency (RTSA), and the Compulsory Standards Act.2 These regulations
are aggressively and routinely enforced at numerous police checkpoints, toll plazas, and mobile
roadblocks distributed throughout the national highway network.
The most heavily scrutinized physical requirement is the mandatory application of specific
reflective safety tapes, governed strictly by the ZS 266 standard for Reflex-Reflecting Vehicle
Number Plates and safety markings.2 It is a strict legal requirement for any vehicle operating in
Zambia to comply with these visibility standards, which are designed to reduce the high
incidence of nighttime collisions involving poorly lit commercial vehicles and wildlife.53 The law
dictates that a vehicle must have two small, white, rectangular reflective strips affixed securely
to the extreme left and right sides of the front bumper.2 Conversely, the rear bumper must
display two small, red, square reflective strips positioned on the left and right outer corners.2
Authorities highly recommend utilizing honeycomb-patterned retro-reflective material to
ensure maximum compliance with RTSA visibility standards.29
If the primary vehicle is towing a secondary asset, such as a commercial trailer, a boat, or a
tourist caravan, additional specific markings are required. The towing configuration must
display a clearly visible white and red "T-sign"—the white section must be displayed on the front
right-hand side of the trailer, and the red section must be mounted on the rear right-hand side
of the trailer, oriented from the perspective of looking forward.2
Furthermore, all vehicles must carry specialized emergency equipment. The driver must
possess two red warning triangles, which are legally required to be constructed on a sturdy
metal plate base rather than lightweight plastic.2 This specific material requirement ensures
that the triangles remain upright and visible in the strong aerodynamic wake generated by
passing heavy commercial trucks on narrow highways.2 Additionally, any vehicle carrying
auxiliary fuel in external jerry cans—a common practice for overlanders navigating the remote
western and northern provinces—is legally required to possess an easily accessible and fully
functional fire extinguisher.2 Finally, the Zambian Highway Code strictly mandates the use of
seatbelts for the driver and all passengers, with specific height and age requirements dictating
rear-seat usage for children under the age of ten.54 Failure to maintain these physical vehicle
standards not only risks the revocation of the temporary entry privileges but subjects the
driver to immediate, non-negotiable roadside fines.
Strategic Implications and Future Outlook
The procedural architecture surrounding Zambia's Temporary Import Permit framework offers
profound insights into the broader operational realities of international trade, regional
integration, and sovereign risk management within Southern Africa. The digitization of the IBDF
through the ASYCUDAWorld platform represents a monumental administrative shift away from
historical bilateral border friction toward a highly sophisticated system of integrated border
management.16 By forcing the consolidation of immigration data, customs declarations,
environmental levies, and national road tolls into a single, cohesive digital workflow, the
Zambian government is actively attempting to modernize its logistical corridors and reduce
--- PAGE 14 ---
devastating dwell times at notorious chokepoints like the Kazungula and Chirundu borders.18
However, the analysis also reveals inherent structural contradictions within the national policy
framework. While the central government publicly seeks to facilitate seamless regional mobility
to boost the lucrative tourism sector and leverage its geographic position as a continental
transit hub, the simultaneous proliferation of aggressively high national road tolls, localized
municipal council levies, and expanding carbon emission surtaxes introduces substantial,
unavoidable financial friction for logistics operators and tourists alike.40 The dramatic 2026
revisions to the NRFA inland toll rates, which saw costs for heavy commercial vehicles spike to
levels heavily criticized by the transport industry, highlight the intense political and economic
tension between generating the massive domestic capital required for infrastructure survival
and adhering to COMESA and SADC treaties regarding harmonized, affordable transit
corridors.38
Ultimately, Zambia's TIP framework successfully functions as both a gateway for legitimate
regional mobility and a formidable, heavily armed barrier against cross-border automotive
smuggling and revenue erosion. The procedure for importing a foreign-registered vehicle into
Zambia using a Temporary Import Permit is not a mere bureaucratic formality; it is a rigorous,
multi-layered gauntlet that demands absolute adherence to documentation authenticity,
complex financial settlements, and strict physical compliance metrics. For the foreign traveler,
commercial logistics operator, or expatriate entering Zambia, operational success requires
proactive, exhaustive preparation, a deep understanding of the regulatory landscape, and a
recognition of the severe consequences of non-compliance within this highly strategic transit
nation.

Temporary Importation of Foreign-Registered Vehicles
into Zambia
First, an important clarification: there are two distinct regimes depending on your situation,
and people often conflate them under "TIP."
Regime 1: IBDF — Integrated Border Declaration Form
For: tourists, visitors, and persons transiting through Zambia driving their own
foreign-registered vehicle for personal use.
This is the most common scenario — a Zimbabwean, South African, or other SADC national
driving in for a visit.
Procedure
The entry process is done at the border in four steps:
Step 1 — Declaration. On arrival, you provide Customs with your personal information,
vehicle particulars (reg number, engine number, chassis number, make, type, colour), your
travelling details (visitor or transit), and any currency exceeding USD 5,000. You also fill in a
vehicle inspection form.
Step 2 — Assessment. Customs assesses the declaration.
Step 3 — Payment. You make a single payment for all applicable fees and levies.
Step 4 — IBDF Issued. Customs prints and hands you the IBDF and a receipt.
You can also apply in advance online through the ZRA Customs Portal or the TaxOnApp
before reaching the border, which reduces dwell time.
Required Documents
The following are required:
● Valid passport
--- PAGE 2 ---
● Foreign vehicle registration book (logbook), in your name
● Insurance certificate
● Temporary Exportation Permit from the country of export (showing the vehicle has
been formally exported)
● If you are not the registered owner: a written authority/Power of Attorney from the
owner, plus a Police clearance certificate for the vehicle
For Zambians wanting to drive in a foreign-registered vehicle: you must be in possession of
a valid work permit or residence documentation from the country where the vehicle is
registered.
For diplomats: you must prove ongoing diplomatic status with a valid diplomatic identity
card.
Validity Period
Visitor: The IBDF is valid for the period of stay granted by Immigration, up to 30 days. It can
be extended twice at the nearest Customs office — the Customs officer must physically
verify the vehicle and be satisfied there is a justified reason. For visitors whose stay extends
beyond 90 days, a further extension (up to an additional 9 months) may be applied for at
Customs Headquarters, Revenue House, Lusaka.
Transit: Validity is 5 days.
Financials
No customs duty or import tax is payable on the vehicle itself under the IBDF. The
customs/estimated value of the vehicle must be declared, but no duty is charged on it.
The fees that are payable are:
● Carbon Emission Surtax
● Council Levy
● Dam Toll fee (where applicable)
● Road Toll fee
The Carbon Emission Surtax for visiting/transiting vehicles (prorated — not the full annual
import rate) is based on engine capacity. From the 2025 ZRA specific duty schedule:

Penalties
Late exit (leaving after the IBDF has expired) attracts a penalty of K900 per day. Any

seizure under Regulation 83(7) of the Customs and Excise (General) Regulations, 2000.
Regime 2: TIP — Temporary Importation Permit (Form TIP 0001)
For: goods (including vehicles) owned by a person or company resident outside
Zambia, for corporate, developmental, or contractual purposes — not routine tourism.
Who Qualifies
Any person, natural or corporate, is eligible to apply. The goods must be owned by a person
or company resident outside Zambia.
Procedure
Application is made in writing on Form TIP 0001, obtainable from any Customs House. The
application must include:
● Physical address of the applicant (and importer if different)
● Reasons for the temporary importation
● Duration of intended stay
● Identification details for the goods/vehicle
● Terms and conditions of supply if on lease or hire
● Form of security for duties and taxes (where Customs requires it)
Completed forms must be submitted to the Station Manager at the intended Port of Entry,
and must be received at least 5 days before the intended date of arrival.
Documents Required
The application must be accompanied by:
--- PAGE 4 ---
● Copies of invoices (where applicable)
● Temporary exportation documents from the country of exportation
● Relevant permits (where applicable)
● Documents supporting the business transaction (e.g. lease agreement, contracts)
● Ownership documents such as the vehicle registration book
Duration
The maximum period allowed is 12 months. Extensions within that 12-month window may
be granted upon written request to the Commissioner General, with valid reasons. The total
period of all extensions combined, including the initial period, cannot exceed 12 months.
Duties Under TIP
Some goods attract partial duties and taxes, others do not. Goods admitted with partial
conditional relief include those imported under hire, lease, or contract, and goods for
commercial use other than professional equipment. For goods imported under related party
arrangements or by a contractor for use on a contract in Zambia, the value for duty purposes
is based on a depreciated value proportionate to the duration of stay — the maximum
lifespan for depreciation is 5 years.
Goods qualifying for total conditional relief include goods for display at exhibitions,
professional equipment, and goods for educational/scientific/cultural purposes.
A security deposit (bond or bank guarantee) covering the full potential duty may be
required by Customs as a condition of approval.
Penalty for Overstay
Any goods that remain in Zambia beyond the authorized period are liable to full duties plus
a fine of 3,000 fee units per day or part thereof. Since April 2024, the value of a fee unit
was increased to K0.40 (40 ngwee) under S.I. No. 25 of 2024. That means the overstay fine
under TIP is K1,200 per day.

● No sale, offer for sale, lease, hire, lending, pledging, or disposal of the vehicle to any

● The vehicle must be removed from Zambia by IBDF expiry or at the time the visitor
leaves, whichever comes first
● In case of breakdown, theft, or accident, the IBDF holder must report to the
nearest Customs office immediately

The importation of motor vehicles into the Republic of Zambia represents a highly regulated,
multi-agency logistical and financial undertaking. Due to the absence of a large-scale domestic
automotive manufacturing base, Zambia relies heavily on the importation of both new and used
vehicles to sustain its transport, agricultural, mining, and private sectors.1 To manage this influx,
the government has established a stringent regulatory architecture governed primarily by the
Zambia Revenue Authority (ZRA), the Zambia Compulsory Standards Agency (ZCSA), and the
Road Transport and Safety Agency (RTSA).1
This report provides an exhaustive, forensic analysis of the statutory costs, tariffs, penalties,
and administrative fees associated with importing a motor vehicle into Zambia for the
2025–2026 fiscal cycle. Furthermore, it deconstructs the specific regulatory and logistical
treatments applied to vehicles originating from seven primary global supply markets: Japan,
Thailand, Singapore, South Korea, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), the United Kingdom (UK),
and South Africa. By integrating customs valuation models, pre-shipment inspection mandates,
border transit tolls, and domestic registration fees, this document serves as a definitive
architectural guide for calculating precise landed cost estimates.
Part I: The Zambia Revenue Authority (ZRA) Taxation
Architecture
The Zambian customs regime for motor vehicles operates on a bifurcated system. The
methodology used to calculate import duties is determined exclusively by the age of the
vehicle at the time of importation. Vehicles under two years of age are subjected to an Ad
Valorem tax structure based on their market value, whereas vehicles aged two years and older
are assessed using a fixed "Specific Duty" matrix based on the vehicle's physical
characteristics.4
The Ad Valorem Taxation Model (Vehicles Under 2 Years Old)
For new or near-new vehicles (defined as under 24 months from the date of manufacture), the
ZRA employs the traditional Ad Valorem method. This system requires the establishment of a
Value for Duty Purposes (VDP), which is the Zambian Kwacha (ZMW) equivalent of the total
Cost, Insurance, and Freight (CIF) value up to the Zambian port of entry.5
Under the Ad Valorem framework, taxes are not flat; they are calculated sequentially and
compound upon one another. The taxation sequence is executed as follows:
1. Customs Duty: This is the primary tariff barrier levied as a percentage of the CIF value.
--- PAGE 2 ---
The standard statutory rates are 25% for passenger sedans and hatchbacks, 30% for
sport utility vehicles (SUVs) and station wagons, 20% for pickup trucks, and 15% for
heavy commercial vehicles and passenger buses.6
2. Excise Duty: This is a consumption tax applied to the sum of the CIF value plus the
assessed Customs Duty. Standard excise rates range from 0% for certain commercial or
agricultural applications to 30% for high-displacement luxury passenger vehicles. A
critical regulatory update in the 2025/2026 national budget reduced the excise duty on
new hybrid motor vehicles from 30% to 15%, a policy maneuver designed to incentivize
the adoption of environmentally friendly transport and reduce national greenhouse gas
emissions.8
3. Value Added Tax (VAT): A flat statutory rate of 16% is charged on the final taxable value.
In the Ad Valorem system, this taxable value is the aggregate sum of the CIF value, the
Customs Duty, and the Excise Duty.6
The compounding nature of the Ad Valorem system means that high-value new vehicles face a
severe exponential tax burden. When all cumulative multipliers are applied, the total tax liability
can easily exceed 60% to 80% of the vehicle's original purchase price.1
The Specific Duty Taxation Model (Vehicles 2 Years and Older)
Historically, the Ad Valorem system proved highly vulnerable to customs fraud when applied to
used vehicles. Importers frequently engaged in invoice manipulation and under-declaration to
minimize their tax liabilities, leading to substantial revenue leakage for the state.1 To counteract
this, the ZRA transitioned to a "Specific Duty" regime for all motor vehicles aged two years and
older.13
The Specific Duty is a flat, non-negotiable statutory figure assessed purely on the vehicle's
engine capacity (in cubic centimeters), body type, and age bracket.2 It entirely disregards the
purchase price or the CIF value of the vehicle.
A critical analytical distinction must be made regarding the Specific Duty: it is an aggregated
tax. The statutory figure gazetted by the ZRA is a consolidated sum that includes the Customs
Duty, the Excise Duty, the Value Added Tax (VAT), the Motor Vehicle Fee, the ASYCUDA
Processing Fee, and the Motor Vehicle Surtax.15 Importers do not calculate VAT or
administrative processing fees on top of these specific figures; the liability is singular and fixed.
The application of Specific Duties acts as a profound economic equalizer within the automotive
market. Because the tax is decoupled from the vehicle's market value, a premium luxury used
vehicle and a budget economy used vehicle of the identical age, body type, and engine size will
pay the exact same import duty to the ZRA.14 This structural dynamic heavily incentivizes the
importation of higher-trim, well-maintained vehicles, as the effective tax rate as a percentage
of the vehicle's total value decreases the more expensive the vehicle is.

Part III: Mandatory Pre-Shipment Inspections and
ZCSA Compliance
Beyond the financial taxation administered by the ZRA, the Republic of Zambia enforces
rigorous physical standards on used vehicle imports. This mandate is enforced by the Zambia
Compulsory Standards Agency (ZCSA) in conjunction with the Zambia Bureau of Standards
(ZABS), designed to mitigate the dumping of substandard, environmentally hazardous, or
stolen vehicles.3
The Roadworthiness Inspection (RWI) Framework
Under Zambian Standard ZS 560, all used motor vehicles destined for use within the Republic
must undergo a mandatory Pre-Shipment Roadworthiness Inspection (RWI) prior to export
from their country of origin.17 The inspection verifies multiple compliance metrics to ensure
public health, safety, and environmental protection:
● Identification Integrity: Inspectors confirm that the physical Vehicle Identification
Number (VIN) and engine numbers match the export documentation. This is a primary
safeguard preventing the entry of stolen property into the Zambian market.16
● Mechanical Roadworthiness: Comprehensive testing of braking systems, suspension
integrity, steering linkage, and tire tread depth to ensure the vehicle is structurally
sound.16
● Environmental Safety: Assurance that the vehicle is free of prohibited CFC (R12)
refrigerant gases and meets baseline tailpipe emission standards.20
● Radiation Testing: Mandatory screening for radioactive contamination. This became a

Part VI: Source Market Analysis – The Seven Target
Countries
Zambia's regulatory framework applies uniformly at the border, but the logistical pathways, tax
liabilities, and inspection requirements vary dramatically depending on the vehicle's country of
origin. The treatment of the seven specific source markets requested by the inquiry highlights
the operational realities of global automotive arbitrage and the profound impact of origin on
landed costs.
1. Japan
Japan is the undisputed dominant source of used vehicles entering Zambia. This dominance is
driven by the high turnover of the Japanese domestic market, strict local maintenance laws
(the Shaken system), and the mass availability of right-hand-drive (RHD) stock that seamlessly
matches Zambian road regulations.10
● ZRA Taxation Treatment: Vehicles from Japan receive no preferential tariff treatment.
They are subjected strictly to the Ad Valorem system (if under 2 years old) or the
standard Specific Duty matrix (if 2 years or older).2
● ZCSA Compliance & Inspections: Japan boasts the highest concentration of
ZCSA-appointed agents globally, with JEVIC, EAA, and ATJ all operating extensive
networks.19 Securing an RWI is a seamless procedure costing $140 USD.23 Because the
inspection infrastructure is so robust, Japanese imports rarely trigger destination
penalties at the Zambian border.

2. Thailand
Thailand is a globally critical manufacturing hub for heavy-duty pickup trucks and commercial

3. Singapore
Singapore represents a highly lucrative niche market for high-specification, meticulously

● ZRA Taxation Treatment: Luxury vehicles from Singapore generally feature large engine
displacements. Consequently, they attract the highest tier of ZRA Specific Duties. A
3,000 cc luxury sedan older than 5 years will attract ZMW 46,902.40 in Specific Duty,
alongside the maximum Carbon Emission Surtax.2
● ZCSA Compliance & Inspections: Singapore has an established ATJ and JEVIC
presence.17 Importers must pay the standard $140 USD RWI fee prior to loading. Vehicles
arrive fully compliant, successfully bypassing the destination penalties.23
● Logistics & Routing: Singapore functions as a major maritime hub, allowing for rapid
Ro-Ro transit directly to Dar es Salaam or Durban, making it a highly efficient route for
luxury acquisitions.26

4. South Korea
South Korea is a rapidly emerging source for reliable, affordable SUVs and commercial panel

● ZRA Taxation Treatment: Vehicles from South Korea face standard Ad Valorem or

5. United Arab Emirates (UAE)
The UAE, specifically the Jebel Ali Free Zone in Dubai, acts as a massive global automotive

● ZRA Taxation Treatment: Standard Ad Valorem or Specific Duties apply based on the

have been cheaply converted to RHD for export to Africa. The ZCSA agents (JEVIC/ATJ)

modifications during the RWI to ensure the conversion meets Zambian safety standards.
If the conversion is deemed substandard, the vehicle will fail the RWI and cannot be
legally exported to Zambia.16

● ZRA Taxation Treatment: Due to the prevalence of high-capacity engines in UK exports,

● ZCSA Compliance & Inspections: The UK has authorized agents (JEVIC, ATJ) operating

● Inspection Fees and Prerequisites: The UK is the only market globally where the

7. South Africa (The Dual-Origin Framework)
South Africa presents the most complex regulatory scenario within the entire Zambian
importation matrix due to its geographical proximity, its status as a continental transit hub, and
its membership in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Free Trade Area.39
South Africa serves two entirely distinct functions for the Zambian automotive market, each
triggering vastly different tax and inspection treatments:
Function 1: South Africa as a Transit and Export Hub for Asian Stock Used vehicles from
Japan, Singapore, or the UK are frequently shipped to the Port of Durban, moved into bonded
warehouses, and subsequently transported by commercial car carrier to the Zambian border at
Chirundu or Livingstone.26
● Compliance: South Africa has ZCSA-appointed agents (JEVIC/ATJ) to conduct
inspections on these vehicles before they cross into Zambia. The fee is the standard $140
USD.21
● Taxation: Crucially, these vehicles are subjected to the standard ZRA Specific Duties and
Ad Valorem tariffs. Because they originated in Asia or Europe, they enjoy no preferential
tariff treatment, regardless of the fact that they transited through South Africa.
Function 2: South Africa as a Country of Manufacturing Origin South Africa possesses a
robust domestic automotive manufacturing sector, producing models for Toyota, Ford, BMW,
and Volkswagen within its own borders. Under the SADC Free Trade Agreement, goods wholly
produced or having undergone substantial manufacturing in a SADC member state qualify for
preferential tariff treatment.40
● The SADC Exemption: To qualify, the manufacturing process in South Africa must
account for a minimum of 35% local value addition.41 If a Zambian importer purchases a
vehicle manufactured in South Africa that meets this threshold, they must obtain a valid
SADC Certificate of Origin.39 Upon presentation of this certificate to the ZRA at the
border, the vehicle qualifies for a 0% Customs Duty rate.39
● Strategic Limitation: It is paramount for analysts and importers to recognize the limits of
this treaty. The 0% rate applies only to the customs duty component of the import tax.42
The importer remains fully liable for the domestic Excise Duty, the 16% VAT, and the
--- PAGE 14 ---
Carbon Emission Surtax.11 Furthermore, the SADC Certificate of Origin strictly cannot be
applied to second-hand vehicles that were manufactured in Japan or Europe and merely
registered or driven in South Africa for a few years before being exported to Zambia. The
origin is defined by manufacturing geography, not the jurisdiction of previous
ownership.41
Strategic Conclusion and Landed Cost Synthesis
The architecture of Zambian vehicle importation throughout the 2025 and 2026 fiscal periods
reflects a deeply protectionist and revenue-maximizing posture by the State.1 The transition
from Ad Valorem to Specific Duties for used vehicles has fundamentally eradicated subjective
valuation disputes at the border, replacing them with a highly predictable, mathematically rigid
taxation matrix.1
For automotive logistics planners, private importers, and commercial dealerships, successfully
estimating landed costs requires a holistic integration of multiple agency frameworks. To
accurately project the final cost of an imported vehicle, an importer must sequentially
calculate: the initial FOB price in the source country, the maritime freight and insurance costs to
establish the CIF value, the $140–$200 ZCSA pre-shipment inspection fee (if operating in an
appointed jurisdiction) or the ZMW 2,500–3,500 non-compliance penalty (if sourcing from
unlisted countries like Thailand or South Korea), the $350–$500 port handling charges in
Durban or Dar es Salaam, the ZMW 500 ASYCUDA penalty if pre-clearance is delayed, the
precise ZRA Specific Duty based on engine capacity and age, the ZMW 50,000–200,000 initial
Carbon Surtax, the NRFA border tolls, clearing agent acquittal fees, and finally the domestic
RTSA registration and road tax levies.
Understanding the exact geographical footprint of the ZCSA's inspection agents and the tariff
implications of the SADC Free Trade Area allows importers to execute highly accurate financial
forecasts, optimizing their supply chains and completely mitigating the risk of vehicle
confiscation or capital stranding at the Zambian frontier.
